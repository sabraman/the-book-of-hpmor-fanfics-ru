# Chapter 12: Aftermath

<div id="seg-0270__storytext-9c93142583" class="calibre4">

**A/N:** For a number of reasons, I've decided to drop the monthly posting schedule for this story. I'll still work on it when I feel like doing so - I'm not abandoning it - but I'm no longer committing to regular updates. When I do post new chapters, though, I'll post them on the first of the month.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Aurors hastily turned and held the crowd safely away from the falling stones, Transfiguring a long pole for the purpose - Quirrell was definitely beyond reach, and there was no point letting anyone get hurt by falling stones. Back in the Great Hall, teachers and older students were already finding and helping those hurt by the rain of spells that Quirrell had cast behind him as he fled. Harry dropped the shield within which he and Hermione had been sheltering, but they sensibly stayed out of the way - they didn't need help, and they weren't really able to provide any themselves.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dumbledore found them sharing a profound silence in a shadowed corner of the Great Hall. Harry's arm was around Hermione protectively, and their gazes didn't seem to be registering the ceaseless activity before them. Albus stopped short, and sighed regretfully.

"Can we help you, Professor?" asked Harry listlessly.

Albus took a deep breath. "Harry, I wanted to say how deeply sorry I am that it came to this. I tried to warn you, I know, but I can't shake the feeling that I should have done something to prevent this."

"True," said Harry, "you did warn me that I would lose my mentor."

Hermione turned and wrapped her arms around Harry, who returned the hug. They stood thus for some time, seemingly oblivious to all around them, before Harry spoke again.

"Did you ever lose a mentor like this, Headmaster?"

Albus paused, suppressing a wince. "Yes. Yes, I did once lose a mentor like this."

Harry continued to stare into the distance, but Hermione turned toward her Headmaster inquiringly. He sighed again as he wordlessly raised a privacy charm, but he chose to answer her.

"I suppose he was more a friend than a mentor, as we were much the same age, but I did look up to him. He was so bright, and so curious, and so very powerful. He... He was just beautiful. I couldn't help but believe that he was the man I wanted him to be. Truthfully, I am still ashamed that it took me so long to harden my heart and truly stand against him. I shall carry that regret to the grave, I fear."

Harry suddenly twitched, violently, and opened his mouth to speak. Hermione, far quicker on the uptake in this instance, quietly held a finger to his mouth. Confused, he shut it.

"Miss Granger," said Dumbledore, "words truly cannot express how thankful I am that Harry appears to have exchanged Quirinus for yourself. I am not exaggerating in the slightest when I say that the very fate of the Wizarding world may well turn on that one change."

Somewhat to the old man's surprise, Hermione grinned in response to that - he couldn't know, of course, of her earlier musings which he had thus evoked.

Eventually Harry broke the silence once more. "What do we do now, sir? How soon can the wards be changed to keep Quirrell out?"

"I'm sorry," said Dumbledore, "but the wards were never designed to keep specific individuals out. I can easily lock the castle down and bar anyone from entering, but I have no easy way to do that just for one person."

"Right," said Harry decisively. "Can we work on that, Headmaster? You, me, Hermione, Professor Flitwick, and whoever else is likely to be useful - you'll know that much better than we do."

Dumbledore rocked back on his heels at the sheer chutzpah of that demand, but he did force himself to truly consider the idea. He was satisfied with the Vows that Harry and Hermione had both taken, and he would still have control over any changes. His earlier concerns about Harry's dark potential had been allayed by Harry's oath to Hermione. Really, there was no longer any reason not to take the boy up on his offer.

"Very well," said the Headmaster. "follow me."

Harry and Hermione scurried after the Headmaster as he strode around the Hall; he told Professor McGonagall to take charge (which she had already done), and he collected Professors Flitwick and Babbling before leading them all through an unobtrusive wooden door in one wall of the Great Hall. Once through this, he turned to Harry.

"My office, Harry."

With that the old man vanished in a soundless burst of flame, taking the other two professors with him. A moment later, Bentham took Harry and Hermione away also.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

"Welcome," said Dumbledore as Harry and Hermione arrived in his office. "I have just sealed my office. With the sole exception of phoenix travel, nothing and no one can enter or leave until I release the seal. In the unlikely event that anyone were to peer in the windows from outside, they would see this room unoccupied and undisturbed. Had anyone somehow hidden in here before I invoked the seals, the wards would have notified me. I mention all this to impress upon you the seriousness and secrecy of our present task - you will speak of it to no one without my leave. Agreed?"

"Yes sir," said Harry and Hermione in near-perfect unison, and noted with surprise that Professors Flitwick and Babbling did similar - apparently this was also new to the professors. Dumbledore nodded, satisfied, and waved his guests to places around the walls as he approached the desk in the centre of the room. He tapped the desk with his wand, and spoke some spell or password - the utterance was masked from everyone else by a momentary humming shimmer in the air around his face.

The ancient oaken desk, complete with assorted papers and gadgets strewn across its surface, seemed somehow to roll in on itself. Although its surface had been rectangular a moment before, now it rolled itself up in a large ring which then sank into the floor. For a moment it looked like nothing more than an ancient wooden annulus set into the floor, contrasting bizarrely with the pleasant patterned carpet around it and the unsuspected polished black stone surface within it.

Then the disc of polished rock began to rise from the floor, spreading out as it did so to resemble nothing so much as an obsidian mushroom. Dumbledore laid his hands on the glossy surface, and glowing runes in a variety of colours gradually emerged from its undefined depths.

"Only the Headmaster of Hogwarts can do this, you know," said Dumbledore. "The stone will ignore any other touch. Once the runes are brought forth, however, they will respond to any touch - please understand the power in this device, and the potential for mishaps.

"This is the master wardstone for Hogwarts. To my knowledge there is nothing else like it either here or in any other place, and its mere existence is a secret known to very few people - at this moment, none save the five of us here. Indeed, even Filius had not yet seen it before now."

He smiled. "What you must understand to begin with is that this bears scant resemblance to the practice of Ancient Runes anywhere outside of this room. This is a system devised by the collective genius of the Founders - used nowhere else, and kept a closely-guarded secret for eight centuries.

"Ancient Runes, as taught by Bathsheda here and as studied by Filius and myself, is an art of finely nuanced shades of meaning subtly modifying one another. The twenty-four runes of Elder Futhark are essentially all mixed together, like combining flavours in a soup. It is a fascinating art, to be sure, but one which inherently lacks precision. This is compensated somewhat by the under-appreciated role that intent plays in activating runesets, but the possibilities are still distinctly limiting once one comes to consider the sheer complexity of a task like setting up permanent wards for Hogwarts. The Founders together invented a more complex and capable system, though still using the original twenty-four runes of power. Their crucial innovation was to allow for structure - a cluster of runes can combine to produce a meaning which is then combined with others, and the runes themselves don't influence other runes in different clusters. I explain this because otherwise this whole process would be entirely baffling to you."

Harry nodded to himself, seeing immediately the obvious analogy with computer programming. Although he hadn't done much actual programming himself, he was familiar with the principles - in the Founders' system he recognised abstraction and modularisation, and he began to realise the sheer power laid out before him. He smiled, but said nothing.

As no one else spoke up, the Headmaster continued after a time.

"Now, as for the wards themselves. As I said, this is the master runestone. It is the heart and soul of these wards, and its creation is a feat of magic that is utterly beyond me. It is linked to all the other stones which... I suppose you would say, *produce*, the wards of Hogwarts. There are boundary stones buried two fathoms deep around the perimeter, spaced perhaps a hundred yards apart; these are identical to one another, and are essentially slaves to the directions of this stone here. There is a foundation stone in a cavern deep beneath the castle, and a capstone in the roof of this very tower - they define the lower and upper bounds of the wards, though they themselves do still lie within the wards by a matter of some feet. So far so good. The truly terrifying thing about these wards, however, lies in the other stones - the stones of the castle itself. These are not runestones precisely, but they absorb and store magical power. They constantly absorb power from the ley lines which cross beneath us, and they also absorb power from the magic used by all of us within the castle. Whatever magic dissipates within the castle - a mis-aimed spell which hits a wall, or a spell which fizzles out before it reaches its target, even the remnants of a successful spell - that magic is absorbed and stored in the stones of the castle. I do not know any limit for this storage of power, even if I knew a way to express it - suffice it to say that the power held within the stones of Hogwarts at this moment is more than sufficient to utterly destroy any army that I have yet seen. Understand, this is one reason why I step very carefully in tampering with these wards - one careless mistake, and this entire valley could cease to be. And yes, Mr Potter, I would indeed use this power to destroy any army foolish enough to attack Hogwarts directly. Alas, the Death Eaters were never so honourable as that."

Harry nodded slowly, his mind racing. "So Headmaster, don't for one second think I'm not utterly fascinated by this or that I'm not going to ask you a million questions as soon as I get the chance, but first can we lock down the wards and figure out how to block Professor Quirrell from coming back? He may want to kill me and he definitely wants to kill Hermione, and that does not make for good odds if we let him back in."

Dumbledore nodded and concentrated intensely on the stone beneath his hands, and complex rune clusters rose from its depths to meet his fingertips. "Beneath my left hand is - well, I suppose you might call it a lock. It is part of the vast runeset which constitutes the Hogwarts wards, and it is designed as an attachment point for the rune cluster that you see under my right hand. That, to continue the analogy, is sort of an anti-key - I shall connect it, and that connection will cause the wards of Hogwarts to go into lockdown. Nothing and no one can enter or leave Hogwarts while this is in place, with the exception of phoenix travel - I know of no way to block that, and in any case I do not believe that there exist any other phoenixes today who would consent to transport humans."

Leaving his left hand on the runes that he had indicated as the attachment point, Dumbledore raised his wand in his right hand and drew it across the stone's surface from the anti-key cluster to the attachment cluster. The wand's tip left a trail of glowing white, which sank down into the stone and disappeared with the runes when he lifted his left hand.

"It is done," he said quietly. "I prefer not to so isolate Hogwarts and all who dwell here, even for a short time, but I cannot risk the safety of my students. Now, let us see what may be done to block Professor Quirrell in particular."

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Perhaps an hour later, after Harry and Hermione had received a highly intensive lesson in the rune system of the Hogwarts Four, the five people in the office collectively worked out how to bar Professor Quirrell from Hogwarts - pull his particular Mannaz/Ansuz/Zith (man/mouth/bind) rune cluster from the memory of the wards, and use it to modify the anti-key cluster such that the wards would now bar only him. The solution was far from elegant, but it would work - unless he managed somehow to change his magical signature beyond recognition, Professor Quirrell could not enter Hogwarts without first bringing down the wards. Harry was quite eager to transport Quirrell to a secure holding cell the moment he touched the wards, but they agreed that this was far too difficult to arrange immediately. Harry had also raised the possibility that Quirrell might have an Animagus form; Dumbledore had given him a searching look in response, but had then said that a wizard's magical signature was affected very little by the Animagus transformation.

When the wards had been modified and Hogwarts was no longer either vulnerable or entirely sealed, Harry turned to Dumbledore shamefacedly.

"Headmaster, I'd like to apologise again for how I behaved in our earlier disagreement, and also even for the friendlier discussion with Hermione - I had no idea just how difficult all of this is, or how dangerous."

The old man smiled serenely. "No matter, Harry, no matter - you weren't to know."

Harry looked thoughtful. "Do you happen to have any notes or anything about this rune system, so that Hermione and I could study them on our own time?"
Dumbledore paused for quite a while at this, and he gave Harry a searching look as he answered. "You must understand, Harry, the tremendous importance of keeping this knowledge a secret."

Harry looked sceptical, but answered politely enough. "I don't necessarily agree with you on that, sir, but I will bow to your wishes and keep the knowledge hidden until you say otherwise. I have a workspace and hiding place that is utterly unreachable, except with the aid of a phoenix who knows the way."

The Headmaster's eyebrows shot up almost comically. "You have already created such a retreat for yourself? Harry, you have had a phoenix less than a day!"

Harry smiled a little smugly, but said nothing. However much more he might appreciate Dumbledore now, he certainly wasn't about to give away the secret of his and Hermione's hideout.

After a long and thoughtful pause, Dumbledore nodded decisively. With a tap of his wand he set the master wardstone to packing itself away, before turning back to Harry.

"Very well, Harry. I shall place my trust in you."

Harry bowed - it just seemed the appropriate thing to do. "Thank you, Headmaster."

When the Headmaster's desk had been restored to its usual position, Dumbledore rapidly traced a sequence of rune-shapes on its surface with his wand - of them Harry recognised only Algiz, protection. A small door appeared, set into the tabletop. This Dumbledore opened gently - after some careful rummaging he pulled out a sheaf of ancient-looking papers, which he laid delicately on the desk. A wave of his wand and another sheaf of papers appeared in his free hand, identical in size but seeming brand new. He tossed them on the desk and gently returned the originals to the secret compartment, whose door began to fade from existence the moment he let it drop. Picking up the new sheaf, Dumbledore turned to Harry.

"These are the notes of the Founders Four, on their researches into runic magic. They must surely have had rough workings and test pieces that they did not think valuable enough to preserve for us, but be warned that even these collected notes were not written for the benefit of any save those four - in other words, do not be surprised if you can make very little of them at first."

He paused for a moment, then looked embarrassed. "I'm afraid they're written in medieval Latin. You may wish to learn something of that language."

Harry seemed somewhat disconcerted by this revelation, but Hermione just looked more excited as she practically snatched up the papers. "Thank you, Professor!"

The old man bowed gracefully to her, and then she and Harry took their leave.
