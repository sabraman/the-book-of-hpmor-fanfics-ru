# Chapter 1

This is fanfiction of [Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality](https://t.umblr.com/redirect?z=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.hpmor.com&t=OWEzMmE5NWM3NGEwMWEwYjYwMGZmNDNiYWUyYTRiNTI1ODI2NzJjNSxjSFlsVXBDeA%3D%3D&b=t%3AOzdy9cGasMBtt1VNscqaIw&p=https%3A%2F%2Fsuperdupervenience.tumblr.com%2Fpost%2F92865230741%2Fone-level-deeper&m=1), which is the most important fiction I’ve ever read.

“Mr. Potter, you’ve been standing out there for nearly five minutes,” Professor Quirrell called through his foreboding office door. “You’re worse than Miss Granger. And you are about to be late to my meeting. I do not like when people are late to my meetings.”

The door opened, and Harry took a few steps back from the highly amused harbinger of Doom–who, strangely, seemed to have left his doominess elsewhere today. “Whatever you fear, young Ravenclaw, it cannot be more frightening than earning my animosity.” He returned to his office chair.

“That,” Harry conceded, “is a most compelling argument.”

“Yes.” Harry stopped in the doorway for a few moments, dumbfounded by what he was seeing. At first he thought they must be in a totally different part of the castle. But no, it was the same office he’d been in before. Only each detail had been adjusted to create an atmosphere that clashed with everything he knew about professor Quirrell. It seemed… welcoming. Comforting, even.

The lighting was soft. A hint of musky incense wafted toward the door, and the air was warmer than in the hall outside. The bookshelves were draped in translucent green fabric, obscuring all but the faint outlines of their contents. The furniture was more appropriate for curling up with a novel than for private lessons. A thick fluffy carpet covered the marble floor. The few knick knacks had been removed, and the desk was clear save for a small unlit candle, perfectly centered.

“Mr. Potter,” said Quirrell, smiling, “sit.” He gestured toward the overstuffed armchair opposite him across the desk. Harry complied, and was startled again when the chair adjusted beneath him, hugging him gently. He got the disturbing impression it was relieved to be sat upon.

“You seem ill at ease, Mr. Potter. Is something the matter?” Quirrell looked genuinely concerned–indeed, now that Harry considered it, his manner actually matched the room to some extent. He found that far more disturbing than the chair.

“Well, no, not exactly,” he replied slowly. Harry struggled to articulate his thoughts, shifting nervously. The chair readjusted accordingly.

“You are wondering, among other things, why I seem to have taken decorating advice from Professor Trelawney.” Quirrell was speaking softly in a particularly low register, and he seemed to be taking extra care of his vowels. The effect was… well, it was soothing, actually.

Harry had imagined many possible scenarios unfolding today, but this was definitely not one of them. “The usual atmosphere of my office is not conducive to the purpose of this particular meeting,” the professor explained. “Many believe occlumency must be learned by brute force, that a mind repeatedly assaulted will learn to defend itself more quickly than one tenderly guided. This is false. It is merely difficult for the foolish and impatient to take a more subtle approach.”

“You’re going to… to ‘tenderly guide’ my mind?” Harry barely kept his jaw from hanging open.

The corners of the Professor’s lips twitched upward. “Yes.” He sat back in his own chair, which had not been replaced with an overstuffed sentient cushion.

There was a long pause in which Quirrell was infuriatingly smug, and Harry was trying to stop his head from backflipping so violently. He ran through several reality checks to ensure he wasn’t asleep before concluding that the real world really was out to lunch.

“As much as I’m enjoying this,” mused Quirrell, “I’m afraid I’m going to need you to stop trying to not be confused. We’ll get nowhere if you insist on making sense of everything that happens today. You’re welcome to attempt to sort things out later. Understand that I wear many hats, and leave it at that for now. At the moment, I need you to let your guard down. Will you do that for me, Harry?”

Harry pondered the request for a long time. Professor Quirrell was the last person on Earth around whom Harry felt it wise to let his guard down, especially in light of his recently acquired knowledge that Quirrell was a telepath. On the other hand, if Quirrell wanted to harm him or violate his privacy, it wasn’t like Harry had much say, guarded or not. That was, after all, the point. Finally, he answered: “I’ll give it a try.”

“Splendid. Before we begin, I’ll need your word not to speak of this with anyone. The particular instructional techniques I’ll employ may evidence that my abilities as a legilimens are rather a lot more advanced than even Dumbledore suspects. As I’m sure you’ll understand, I’d like to keep it that way.”

Harry nodded. “Of course. You have it.”

“Good. Now then. It will put you at ease to know that I will not be reading your mind. Not in the way you fear, anyway. A sufficiently skilled legilimens can detect the structure of thoughts without intruding upon their contents. I shall monitor abstract patterns and emotional overtones, but that is all the information I’ll need to guide you effectively, at least at this stage. I’ll continue to obtain whatever other information I desire the hard way. Do you understand?”

“You’ll know how I think, but not what I think.”

“Precisely. In addition, remember that legilimency generally is not mind control. I will not force you directly to think or behave according to my will.”

Harry tilted his head hesitantly. “What exactly do you mean by 'directly’?”

The Professor pondered this for several seconds–an unusually long time for him–before answering. “The choice to follow my instructions will always be yours. Your thoughts will remain entirely under your own control.”

Harry smirked at this. “Cognitive science is a very new field, but we understand enough already to know it doesn’t work the way you’re suggesting.”

Quirrell seemed puzzled, so Harry went on.

“Come to think of it, we hardly need to look much farther than philosophy here. Let’s say something is my choice if–and only if–my wanting it to happen causes it to happen. What causes me to want it to happen?”

Harry paused, gathering his thoughts. “Well, that’s probably really complicated, but it at least has something to do with unconscious parts of my mind. The things in my unconscious are there for a whole lot of reasons, none of which are that I am currently deciding for them to be there. By definition, actually. If things I never wanted and caused to happen can affect the overall makeup of my mind–things like where I was born or how long I slept when I forgot to set my alarm–then you can too. Magic or no.”

Now Quirrell looked a little worried.

“I assume,” Harry concluded, “you mean I will retain whatever degree of control over my thoughts I usually have.“

"I’m afraid the degree of control you usually have is the best I can offer. I’m astonished that muggles have discovered such things without the use of legilimency, but we will have to save further discussion of it for another time.”

“Agreed,” said Harry.

“It would not benefit me to harm you, Mr. Potter, or I’d have done it already. You have my word that I will not. Will that do?” Harry nodded his consent. “Let us begin, then.”

“What do I do?” Harry asked. His heartbeat suddenly seemed quite loud.

The lights dimmed further. “First of all, watch this candle flame.” The candle spontaneously ignited. “Keep watching it. You won’t take your eyes off the flame, no matter how relaxed and drowsy it makes you. Other than that, you don’t need to do anything besides what comes naturally.” Harry watched the flame. Professor Quirrell continued speaking, very slowly, and with increasingly long pauses that matched up with Harry’s breathing. The rest of the room around the candle flame quickly faded out. It was as though his visual cortex had simply given up on interpreting any other information.

“The purpose of today’s lesson is to enhance your powers of introspection. As you already know, there are many features of consciousness most people routinely neglect to attend to. The ones we’ll focus on today are called 'surfaces’, and they are the first structures a legilimens encounters when attempting to enter your mind.”

Harry’s heart had slowed down. A lot. Quirrell seemed to be speaking from much farther away than the other side of the desk. Surfaces, thought Harry. Got it.

“This will take a type of concentration you’ve likely never experienced, for which relaxation is essential. Following my instructions will induce that relaxation. But you needn’t be concerned with following my instructions when it does not feel completely effortless to do so. This is not a test–and, I assure you, you will enjoy it immensely.”

It was not effortless to keep watching the candle. In fact, it was becoming quite difficult. His eyes wanted to blink heavily, to drop their gaze from the very bright light. But, strangely, he felt it would have been even more difficult to look away.

“Relax into the chair. You’ll find that no effort whatsoever is required to maintain an upright position. Let go of every single muscle. Besides, of course, your eyelids, however heavy they feel. Keep watching the candle.”

The chair certainly seemed eager to take more of his weight. He obliged, and felt as though he were melting. Which was a surprisingly pleasant sensation. It seemed to be charmed to seek out muscles that were still holding tension. It would then massage them gently until they completely relaxed. He was barely aware of what Quirrell was saying now. It took almost all of his attention to keep his eyes open, to keep watching the candle.

Then, like a very dim star you can see only when you don’t look too hard, he felt something soft at the edge of his mind. He’d never experienced his consciousness as having an edge, or a shape of any kind. But now it seemed to have an outer surface, or at least part of one, and there was a light pressure against it. That part seemed to be in the top left corner toward the back, though that didn’t make any sense. It was confusing, though not unpleasant.

Professor Quirrell’s voice was even quieter now, as quiet as a whisper, yet still low and full. “Soon you will not be able to keep your eyes open,” he intoned. “The impulse to relax will be too overwhelming, and you will simply be incapable of sustaining your gaze any longer.”

At that, the difficulty at least tripled.

“It’s all right, Harry. When you feel that impulse, just let them close.” The pressure on his mind increased, matching the now tremendous weight of his eyelids. Wordlessly, the pressure was giving him permission, seducing him to let go. It was too much. The next time he blinked, he yielded to it, his eyelids finally dropping in delicious surrender. And then the world was gone.

Harry didn’t know for how long he sat in silence after that. It could have been a few minutes. It could have been hours. It was the most profound calm he’d ever experienced.

He could often feel that gentle presence drifting about, alighting now and then on the surface of his thoughts, sinking sometimes to chart the convoluted depths of his mind. It moved with a slow rocking motion, propelled by the wind of his breath. Delicately, it guided him to relinquish his unconscious grasp on each cognitive process still running in the background, leading to deeper relaxation than he’d thought possible.

His mind grew consummately still, until he could not have remembered what it meant to think or to feel anything at all besides this perpetual abandonment to calm. He watched it drift without guessing what would happen next, without caring. He’d completely forgotten he was in Quirrell’s office, that he had a body, that this was all part of a lesson in occlumency. Eventually, even the drifting ceased.

Utter the darkness, empty the void, silent the endless night.

Then, smashing through the silence, a single thought thundered across his mind: “It’s full of stars.” For out of the darkness suddenly burst ten thousand points of light, and he thought he was again observing Professor Quirrell’s secret spell.

Even as the true image of distant space enshrouded him, something seemed discordant. Something was different. This was not quite what he remembered. It took him awhile (though time meant little now), as he basked in its austerity, to make out what had changed.

Oh. He wasn’t seeing it. His eyes were closed, but it wasn’t even a visual image at all. He was feeling those blazing stars against the abyss. They were his awareness cast about in countless burning points of concentration, crystallized thought glittering across his whole consciousness. If there is something it is like, he thought wordlessly, to be the limitless night sky, perhaps this is it. He was not aware of the tears on his cheeks.

And then he heard the professor’s voice, barely above a whisper. “Those points are your own attention. I am now only observing from the outside. You are maintaining them, and you can control them as you please. Try moving one. You’ll find they move along curved planes, if you don’t push too hard.” Harry tried. Nothing happened. “You can’t move them quite like you’d move a limb. You probably won’t experience a strong sense of agency when it happens. Just expect it to happen, and don’t get in its way.”

Harry expected one of the stars to move. He relaxed, letting its path be empty, and simply knew where it would go. Gently, it began to glide, like a satellite across the upper atmosphere, along the surface of his mind. He felt Professor Quirrell indicate another point of light, and he nudged that one to the left. “Try several,” Quirrell instructed. Harry picked a patch of about a dozen stars, and moved them all just a little upward.

“Excellent, Harry. You have found your surfaces. You are very close to making a rudimentary occlumency barrier.” Harry continued sending stars this way and that as Quirrell spoke. He felt like he was simply watching, and that by some incredible coincidence the stars moved as he predicted they would. It was strange to feel a third party to his own actions.

“Relax, now, Harry. Let the points rest where they are.” There was silence again, and Harry sank down a little deeper into the blankness, which he hoped he would never have to leave. “Good. Just keep dropping like that. Don’t worry about the stars; they’re stable enough now that you needn’t tend to them actively.”

Another immeasurable stretch of time passed. He could feel the presence of the Professor again, guiding his thoughts back down to perfect stillness. When next he heard Quirrell’s voice, it was only in his mind.

“The stars are points of intensely concentrated awareness. Imagine they are clenched like fists. Can you feel them struggling, wanting to open?” He paused for Harry to take note of the sensation.

“Now, let them go.” Harry released them all at once, and the lights began to diffuse, to blossom out across his mind.

They melted into little puddles, like ice cubes on hot pavement. The borders of the pools of light met, merged, and reinforced each other. Now broad stretches of sky hung in soft radiance all around him, the stars replaced with what felt like giant forcefields. They were warm and reassuring. As he poured more of himself into them, they became brighter, and Harry felt strong.

Quirrell’s words were soft, but with a hint of excitement. “You have much more in you,” he said. “You can reinforce this barrier five times over at least.” Harry felt Quirrell touching a surface, felt him encountering resistance. It was like answering before the question was over, and he knew the barrier was working.

Without the usual cognitive machinery grinding away, weighing and calculating and inhibiting, his response to his success was immediate, fierce: A colossal dam broke inside of him then. All the certainty, fortitude, and satisfaction he could experience rushed directly into the forcefield. He thought he might have outshone the sun. Harry felt invincible as the barrier blazed across his mind.

“It is enough, Harry,” Quirrell said. “Remember this sensation, but do not maintain it. Back off now.” It was difficult for him to stem the flow of emotion, but when finally he’d drawn the storm back into himself, Harry was utterly exhausted.

He spiraled downward at an incredible rate, and Quirrell was there to assist in recovery, even as he continued to speak aloud. “That was very well done. Such a display was beyond even my ambitions for your first lesson. You intuited an effective approach without explicit instruction.” His voice was back to low and soothing, and he was speaking again in a rhythm that followed Harry’s breath. “I’m going to start counting up soon from one to ten, and you will begin moving toward regular wakefulness. But not just yet. Rest here awhile.

“You have gained full awareness of your surfaces, and the method you employed has its advantages. But it is also terribly fragile.

“A display like that completely occludes a very brief scan and may even blind the legilimens for a few moments. But any legilimens worthy of that designation would shatter such a barrier completely without thinking twice. All I’d have to do is introduce a seed of self doubt, and it would be over. Not only would I have easy access to the deeper parts of your mind, but you’d have no energy left for further defense.”

There was silence for several minutes, as the professor simply let Harry sit, keeping his mind to himself. Eventually, he started counting, and little by little Harry became aware of his body, the room, and what he was doing in it. On ten, Harry opened his eyes.

He took a breath to speak, but gave up on it right away when he realized he didn’t know what to say. He’d need to spend some time processing these past two hours before he could think straight enough to say much of anything at all. The professor smiled. “You will return at three tomorrow, and I will answer your questions then. That is also when you will receive your first assignment.” Harry nodded rather languidly. “For now, Mr. Potter, have a light dinner, and do not attempt anything more engaging than checkers. You will sleep very soundly indeed this night.”

“Yes,” said Harry, “I expect I will.”
