I would first like to state for the record that I was not very happy with your first attempt at "guess my solution or BAD END", and I am once again disappointed that you would repeat the same old threat at the climax. While downer endings are fashionable at times, there are reasons and logic behind why we read and write fiction, and most of the time, readers don't enjoy seeing the characters who they've mentally struggled alongside crushed meaninglessly just to be contrary. Readers will support the occasional Red Wedding, but go to that well too often, and you're just writing bad Outer Limits stories.

Fine, I'll play, although I'm reasonably certain that my answers, or variations thereof, have already been suggested many times over.

1. Voldemort has miscalculated, due to his one glaring blind spot: he is a perfect sociopath. His personality is purely narcissistic. He is unable or deeply, subconsciously unwilling to empathize with any other human being, or indeed see anyone as more than a toy for his amusement. In his personal viewpoint, all others are playthings, while he is the only true human. That is the power he knows not-not love, exactly, but empathy.

So... who is more likely to destroy the world (and maybe the universe), a boy (or even later as a man) who vows to end death and entropy, but is restrained in his approaches by his innate moral code and his love for his parents and best friend, the latter of which has a nigh-unbreakable conscience (as tested by Voldemort himself), not to mention the Vow he just made tying himself to her will? Or a man who cannot or will not recognize any other as truly human, who is bound to grow bored of his lonely existence within millennia, or even mere centuries?

Indeed, Harry can put forth the claim that the prophecy is targeted at Voldemort, and the timing of it was the point at which he truly defeated Harry-Harry hasn't been operating at full speed mentally since then, and has adopted an almost fatalistic view, making his defeat all but certain. In order for the world to be protected from Voldemort's antipathy, the Dark Lord must either relinquish a large portion of his power/freedom, or all of it (i.e., his life). Explaining this is the ultimate rational victory, as no powers, no tricks, no brute force is required; only words of pure logic.

2. If it must come down to violence, well... "The floor is lava". O.K., that specifically would be a poor solution for a number of reasons, but the concept is sound. Harry's greatest skill is Transfiguration, because it is the most open to whatever the mind can conceive, and Harry can conceive of quite a lot. Partial Transfiguration of the ground, the air, even body parts (for that matter, can Harry Transfigure blood inside another body?) leads to many possibilities, from carbon monowire to poisonous gasses to, well, turning the floor to lava (which would fail, but it would look cool in the process!).

3. "Don't you know that you're already dead?/I did it twenty-five minutes ago." This would seem to violate your rules as written, but I can't deny the possibility that there is a larger trap being sprung-to some extent, Harry hasn't been thinking straight since the public Battle Magic fight, and certainly not since the troll. He went to bed without checking with Hermione or Draco, convincing himself that nothing was wrong. He played beneath his game at the trial. He wasted time not thinking straight when the troll attacked, delaying him. He moped around ever since that day, becoming mentally lethargic, reactive instead of proactive. All of those things could be explained, if nothing else than by Voldemort sabotaging his mind-but, Voldemort hasn't admitted to doing anything of the sort. Something has seemed fishy there for a long time, and doubly so since the note Voldemort sent "back" to Harry contained none of his recognition codes. Could Harry have been Confounded for a long time, or at least, since before he went back in time?

Either one might be enough to save his life, but both together constitute a compelling argument.

First: Why should Voldemort worry that killing Harry could bring about the prophecy? (1) He's been burned by unintentionally fulfilling a prophecy before. See Ch 112 ("'Prophecy I heard of mysself led me to fulfill it. Have not forgotten that dissasster.'"). And there is a LOT more at stake this time. (2) Voldemort heard the prophecy from Trelawny when Hermione was killed. No one else was with them, and he's refused to tell anyone else exactly what was said (see Ch 112, refusing to tell Harry). Ergo, Voldemort is the person who was \*meant\* to hear the prophecy. Compare Ch 86 (Quirrel himself notes that prophecies are said to be "spoken to those with the power to cause them or avert them"). (3) Voldemort's extraordinary steps to defeat the prophecy are so over the top that they invite the suspicion that it will all backfire. I mean, raising Hermione from the dead AND making her invulnerable AND creating a horcrux for her? (4) Voldemort's planned method for killing Harry will be irreversible. Voldemort can always kill Harry at some future date, but he cannot undo this killing afterwards.

Harry needs to know whether the prophecy specifically names him (we know it doesn't, but he doesn't have that information). See Ch 46, where Harry asks the same thing of McGonagall about the "Dark Lord" prophecy. Once Harry knows that he's not named, the next step - not necessary, but useful - is to offer alternative candidates.

Who else could Harry suggest might be the subject of the prophecy?

(1) Hermione. (Yes, I know Hermione is not a "he," but bear with me.) Hermione is also Muggle-raised (so not subject to the mental limitations on those born in the magical world) and smarter than Harry. She's got all his tools, more brains, and she's seen how Harry works. The resurrected Hermione is also darker, by Voldemort's own admission. Voldemort has made her essentially invulnerable, and killing Harry could give her as much incentive to take some sort of action - revenge, challenging death - as her own death gave Harry. In short, a dead Harry could make the new Hermione into an angry super-Harry. Oh, and Hermione not being a "he"? The "he" in the prophecy might be Voldemort, who has formed her into that weapon pointed at creation.

(2) Draco. Harry's started him down the road to rationality. He'll mix that with magic, but lack Harry's sense about it - either common sense or moral sense. And he'll have the Malfoy name and wealth to help him put his plans into effect.

(3) Someone as-yet-unnamed. If Harry isn't the prophesied one, then Voldemort's destroying Harry won't actually stop anything, and it would take Harry out of the game, which is bad. Harry's desire to give everyone immortality makes him an obvious opponent to the Unnamed Baddie. Voldemort might be clearing the field for the Baddie to succeed. Besides which, he's a junior Tom Riddle - wouldn't Voldemort want a younger clone to fight with him against this unknown but common enemy? Plus, if Voldemort persists in killing Harry even after Harry makes this argument, then Voldemort must necessarily be 100% certain that Harry is the danger, and killing him eliminates it - and so Voldemort himself will stop looking for the real threat. (The Unnamed Baddie possibility wouldn't be very convincing from a literary perspective, but to Voldemort and Harry, they aren't in a story. Compare Ch 94 (similar note about the unsatisfying diabolus ex machina of the enemy taking actions out of literary sight).)

How to confirm the prophecy? A person who is the subject of a prophecy - and only that person - can hear it in the Hall of Prophecy at the Department of Mysteries (see Ch 86). So Voldemort should take Harry there. If the glowing orb floats to Harry and lets him hear the prophecy, then Harry will know the prophecy is about him. If Harry can't hear the prophecy, Voldemort will know he needs to worry about someone else. Voldemort could also test Hermione and himself at the same time (himself last, so that he's seen from the example of Harry and Hermione that there's no danger from trying to hear a prophecy not meant for you).

Harry can say all this in Parseltongue because he can truly believe it. He wants to give immortality to everyone; he does not want to destroy the universe, and he's just taken an Unbreakable Vow to keep from doing it. For those reasons, there is a significant chance that he is not the subject of the prophecy.

And Harry himself would want to know. If someone else is the threat, Harry will want to fight them. And if Harry becomes convinced that he himself is the threat, I think he'd be willing to die to stop the prophecy. He would thus be willing to help Voldemort get into the Hall of Prophecy. And so Harry can say all of that in Parseltongue as well.

Now Voldemort has a compelling interest to take a cooperative Harry to the Hall of Prophecy. If it turns out Harry really is the danger, Voldemort can just kill him then. And if Harry isn't, Voldemort will have an ally against the real threat.
