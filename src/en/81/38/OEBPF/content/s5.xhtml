Chapter 4: Luna

The next two train compartments to where the Weasleys were sitting were packed with students, so there was no chance of my sitting there. When I opened the door to the third compartment, the three girls inside were talking seriously in intense whispers. I wasn't sure what to do, and as I stood gaping with the door opened, the one with the lightest colour hair, who wore wizard robes with green trim stood up angrily and forced the door closed in my face, saying something like, "Haven't you ever heard of privacy?"

I was startled and wandered on a little shakily. I opened the next door much more cautiously. The compartment was nearly empty, but I hesitated before entering. The only person in the room was a blond, round-faced, nice-looking girl sitting by the widow. She looked over at me for a moment, and then looked back out the window. This wasn't the most welcoming response, but I was tired of checking doors, and after a moment I said, carefully, "Is... would it be alright if I joined you in here for a while?"

At that, she turned again, and this time smiled warmly, so I came in and closed the door behind me. She still hadn't said anything. We sat in slightly awkward silence for a bit before I got my courage up again, "My name's Colin," I said softly.

"I'm Luna," she said. Her voice was almost eerily calm. She had a chrysanthemum behind each ear and was wearing a quartz necklace.

More silence.

Eventually I broke the silence again, "Erm, so, erm... Have you been to Hogwarts before?"

"Oh, no, I'm a first year." Still that same strange sleepy-tranquil voice.

"Oh, good. Me too." I couldn't believe how my capacity for normal conversation seemed to have suddenly escaped me.

I just sat there for a moment, until finally she seemed to realize something. Then she visibly relaxed and seemed to open up a bit more, "You're Muggleborn, aren't you?" she asked.

"Erm, yes, how did you...?"

"It's obvious, isn't it?"

"Erm, what?"

"Well, everybody in Wizarding Britain knows who I am because of my dad."

I didn't know what to say to that, so just looked at her.

"They all think I'm daft, don't they?"

"Because of your dad?"

"Sort of. Oh, of course I forget. There must be so much that you don't know!" she raised her hand to her mouth in a style I might have seen in an actress in a really old movie one time. Her huge eyes opened even wider.

"Erm, so, your dad is somebody important, then?" I floundered.

"Only the editor of a major newspaper," she reached into her bag, pulled out a copy of The Quibbler, and shoved it toward me, "Most people don't understand what Dad is doing, though."

The typesetting of the front page distinctly reminded me of The Daily Mirror, and the front page headline read, "Fixus Nothingus? Lives Ruined by Mysterious Ritual."

I stared at the headline as she went on, "It doesn't help, of course, that Dad has a sense of humor. When I was younger I used to talk about my dreams a lot. I guess I said some really weird things, but also kind of just the sort of strange things that kids say sometimes, you know?"

When I looked up from the headline and nodded uncertainly, she went on, "Dad would create whole satirical news stories around the things I said. But people didn't understand, and would make fun of him. And now they all think I'm bonkers, of course."

"Oh." I said stupidly. And then because I didn't quite know what else to say, added, "I'm so sorry." I honestly didn't have any idea what I was supposed to say. The whole conversation felt strange. But I found myself liking Luna anyway. Most of our Hogwarts classmates don't think much of her, and a lot of people say that she looks like a frog, describe her eyes as bulging, but I never thought so. She is... peculiar... though.

"Thanks for coming in, by the way," she said.

"It was nothing," I said, "Honestly, I'm glad to find somebody who seems friendly. I was sitting with the Weasleys before, do you know them?"

She nodded her head with a tired look that seemed to say, "Who doesn't?"

"...but they started going on about sport, and it's really not my thing, you know? And then the girls in the compartment next to yours shut me out..."

"Daphne, Susan, and Hermione," Luna cut in, "They shut themselves up in there as soon as the train started accepting passengers. Can't say I blame them. Hermione never asked to be a celebrity, you know. The whole thing is such sad business..."

"Sad? But isn't Hermione..."

"Oh, I don't mean sad for Hermione, although of course it's hard for her being the first person ever to be brought back," Luna hurried on, "I mean for everybody else."

"You mean like the old Headmaster of Hogwarts? Dumble... um... chap? What was his name?"

"Dumbledore? Oh, yes, that's a nasty business too, of course. No, I meant for people less... directly affected. Take poor Professor Trelawney, for instance." She pointed to the headline in the newspaper she'd passed to me, so I picked up the newspaper and settled down to read.

Next to the headline was an enormous photograph of Sybill Trelawney, who gazed off to the left side of the image so intently that it might as well not have been a moving photograph. The article was all about the difficult situation she was in now that she'd been sacked from Hogwarts. It hinted that there were others similarly affected but didn't give any specifics. Ms. Trelawney used to teach an upper level elective called Divination, I learned, but Hogwarts was doing away with Divination entirely, now, and Professor Trelawney didn't know where she'd go or what she'd do.

When I looked up to indicate that I was finished reading, Luna continued, "She has the True Sight, Professor Trelawney. I know. I'm a little touched myself..." she trailed off briefely and then hurried on, "Oh, not in the way they think. I do wish Dad hadn't actually used my name in all of those articles. Anyway, it is most unfortunate that Trelawney won't be staying at Hogwarts."

"The article says that the new Headmistress... McGonnagall, is it?... offered her the option to stay at Hogwarts as unofficial faculty." I wasn't quite sure what to think of Luna at that stage, but decided to keep an open mind. She certainly seemed nice enough.

"That's true," said Luna, "But it's hard enough for Diviners to find their way in the world. So many people say that Divination is the one branch of magic with no real basis. Prophecies are one thing. Everybody knows those are real. But too few wizards and witches fully embrace general intuition, or the fact that most Seers have abilities far beyond their unusual sensitivity to time pressure. It's a loss for us all, not having somebody like Professor Trelawney teaching at Hogwarts. And disrespectful, too, given that it was Seers like her who discovered magic in the first place."

"Oh, did they?" I asked. I still wasn't sure what to think at that point. In some ways I still don't. If I'd grown up in Magical Britain, I probably would have dropped out of the conversation right there. After all, everybody knows that magic was first manipulated in the lost city of Atlantis... and that all Atlantian records have long since been lost. How could someone like Luna have knowledge still lost to wizarding scholars the world over? And yet, in other ways I'm still not sure.

As it was, since I'd been raised by Muggles, I didn't have much basis for evaluating statements made by other witches and wizards, and while I was starting to get the impression there was something off about her, I also wasn't in a position to dismiss the things she said. Looking back, I'm thankful for that. She's been a good friend.

Just at that moment, though, she glanced out the window and started in surprise, then said quickly, "We're almost there! You'd better get into your robes. I'll see you at the Feast!"
