Chapter 11: Homecomings

The Christmas holidays came before I knew it, and as I exited Platform 9 3/4 I was warmly greeted by the whole family. I felt like a hero returning from a great adventure!

Over the holidays, my parents and Dennis asked all sorts of questions: what was Wizard school was like, why we did we need a cauldron, what my favourite classes were, and so on. They were quite impressed when I told them about Flying lessons, and Dennis wanted to know whether I could fly on any old broom. He was disappointed to hear that only magical broomsticks could fly, and was even more disappointed when I explained that we weren't actually allowed to use any magic at all outside of school.

He looked sad about that for a moment, and then frowned, "But what about those moving photographs?"

So I did my best to explain about accidental magic. And then I realized that even though I couldn't do magic, I could still show my family some magical objects. I only had one magical item with me other than my wand, though, and I went to fetch the first issue of the Hogwarts Weekly.

Mum just about bawled when she saw my name, in print, next to the word "photographer." Dennis looked at the cover photo for a bit, and then demanded to know who Harry was and why he was so important. Meanwhile, Dad was very interested read about what kinds of clubs magical students would start.

Questions like these continued throughout the holiday, and I never ran out of things to talk about: the Great Hall with its magical ceiling, the House Elves, the Lake, the Whomping Willow, the talking paintings, and the famous tension between Harry and Granger. I even told them about Science class.

When the holidays ended, I came back to Hogwarts more excited than ever, and the Spring term seemed to fly by. Classes, photography, arguing with Lee and Cedric about the layout for the school newspaper, and of course, Chaos Club.

Over the Spring term, Chaos Club lost a few more students but eventually seemed to stabilize. We continued meeting to work on exercises to help think more rationally, as well as doing a few more experiments, although Granger became an almost perpetual voice of caution whenever magical experiments were proposed. I think Harry would have preferred to move ahead with less argument, but I also slowly realized that he had a ton of respect for Granger, even when they disagreed (which was usually).

Also, Draco seemed to have some sort of crush on Granger, often taking her side even when it seemed he didn't actually understand her arguments. Harry always got annoyed when that happened, and Neville and Padma would roll their eyes and snicker.

There was so much to do, but as the term finally drew to a close, I found I was quite excited to get home again, too. I would have a lot to tell, I knew, and really was looking forward to the sort of welcome I'd gotten at Christmas. I just knew my family would be so excited to hear more about Hogwarts.

I took the long ride to London on the Hogwarts Express, so excited to tell everybody about the Spring term, but it turned out that only Dad was there to pick me up. He'd muttered something about Mum having taken Dennis to the library before driving me home. And while he seemed happy enough to see me, he didn't ask many questions about Hogwarts.

Well, I thought, perhaps Mum and Dennis will be more interested when they get home. Anyhow, I was tired, and headed straight for my bed. But then...

"Colin, you scream like a girl!" Barely home, and there was Dennis, framed in the doorway to our bedroom, laughing like a maniac.

What was I going to say to that? What could I say to that? You'd scream too if you came home and the first thing you saw was a bloody huge snake coiled up on your pillow. That's what I wanted to say, but this was Dennis. Little wanker. He'd be delighted if something like that happened. Of course he would. God, but I miss him, though.

The thing was, though, that since Christmas, while I'd been keeping busy at Hogwarts, things had been happening at home, too. I guess I'd forgotten that life would go on for my family even when I wasn't there.

And now there was a snake on my bed.

I stood with my back pressed against the wall opposite the foot of my bed. I'd barely stepped into the room when I spotted the snake on my pillow and jumped back, almost losing balance as I collided with the wall. I guess I did yell a bit, but I certainly hadn't screamed, no matter what Dennis said. I did have a weird tight feeling in my throat, though.

Dennis must have just got home from the library and had hurried into the room when he heard. Clearly, he thought the whole thing was hilarious.

"Dennis," I finally managed to croak out, "Why?"

The little git just laughed again, then calmly walked over to my bed and picked up the snake. It suddenly didn't look as big as I'd thought at first. Only a little longer than Dennis's forearm, I realized as it coiled around his hand. As he stretched out on his own bed, he spoke softly to the animal and actually stroked it.

I managed to relax a bit, and Dennis glanced up at me, a smug little smile playing at one corner of his mouth, "See, it's safe now," then went on "Nate wouldn't hurt you anyway. She's only a grass snake. But crikey did you yelp."

I edged over to my bed, keeping as far from Dennis's side of the room as I could, then lay down. I wasn't too pleased with putting my head where that slimy beast had been, but it was my bed, darn it.

As I calmed down, Dennis lay with the snake curled on his chest, and told me about how he'd gotten her.

"She's from one of the hedges near Grandma's place in the country. I couldn't just leave her there."

"Dennis, I thought Mum said you shouldn't disturb the creatures that live there. 'They're happiest where they are.' That's what she says every time we're down there." I still wasn't happy about the snake, especially having her on my bed.

Dennis answered right away, though, "Not any more, though, are they?"

That made me sit up. I swung my legs off the edge of my bed and turned to watch Dennis carefully, "What do you mean?"

Dennis looked up in surprise and the dull brown grass snake lifted her head as well. "Blimey," said Dennis, "I guess you haven't heard." He looked at me carefully, then continued, "They're going to bulldoze all those hedgerows at the manor. The new owner wants to put in a big modern farm."

That was a surprise, and not a good one either. I closed my eyes tightly, and images filled my mind. Photographing the fields and hedges from on top of the big rock by the lane. Climbing the big apple tree while Dennis belly-crawled on the ground near the hedges. Walking with Mum and Grandma.

Those hedgerows were hundreds of years old I knew, with all kinds of birds and things nesting in them. Mum and Grandma both loved those fields and hedges like anything. And I'd taken some of my favourite photographs on the manor grounds.

I couldn't believe it. But I was also confused, "I thought the landowner promised never to take those hedges down. Didn't Grandma's group from the town convince him that they were too important to the landscape? I thought he agreed that the manor grounds were worth more left as they are!"

"Well," said Dennis, "I guess he did. But all that is changed now. Something about the new treaty that Prime Minister Major and President Gore put together at that big meeting in Japan. The one about global warming."

"The what now?" I'd heard of global warming. Anthony had told everyone in Chaos Club all about it when he was doing a presentation on solar energy and how magic might improve how solar panels work. Global warming sounded really scary, and I was glad that soon the solar panel designers would have help from the Wizarding community. But Anthony hadn't said anything about any meeting in Japan. We don't talk much about political things in Chaos Club.

"I think the treaty is called the Kyoto Agreement or something. It's supposed to stop global warming."

"What does this have to do with the manor, though?"

Dennis paused, seemed to be trying to remember, then continued slowly, "Well, the climate stuff is mostly about not burning oil and coal and things. But they say some of it is also because they keep cutting down trees, especially rainforests in places like Indonesia and Brazil. The Kyoto Agreement says that countries like England and Europe and America have to burn less coal, but it also says that we have to help other countries protect their rainforests. So they made some kind of system where you can buy and sell permission to burn fuel or cut down trees, so... well... see, the manor owner says that it won't cost him much to cut down the hedges and woods on the grounds, and that he's best off using his land to make money if he also spends some money to protect rainforest in Indonesia."

Dennis looked sad, and I knew just how he felt. The grounds were so beautiful, and I loved thinking about how they'd been like that practically for ever.

But I felt torn, too. Global warming was really scary, and even Dennis had always talked about how important it was not to cut down the jungles in Indonesia because orangutans needed somewhere to live. I felt sad about the hedgerows, but I also realized that I wasn't thinking rationally at all.

I quietly wished I could talk things over with Harry and Anthony, or maybe Draco.
