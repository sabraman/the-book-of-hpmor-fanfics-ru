Chapter 8: Chaos Club

There they were, framed in the doorway: the green-eyed boy with the famous lightning-bolt scar, and the beautiful white-blond boy that I soon learned was Draco Malfoy. Of course, after all I'd heard about Harry Potter-Evans-Verres – the Boy-Who-Lived, developer of Fixus Everythingus, savior of Hermione Granger, and friend of the Minister of Magic – I simply had to take his picture.

But before I could press the shutter button on my camera, Harry spotted the camera and looked straight at me. I froze in surprise, and before I could move again the Boy-Who-Lived to put an expansive arm around Draco, guiding his beautiful friend to look directly into the camera as well. So my first photo of Harry Potter turned out to be a photo of Draco Malfoy too.

This, I now know, was image-control on Harry's part. Harry is playing a long-range game, you see. He knows that even childhood photos of himself and Draco could have small but important consequences for Draco's long term political career. The Malfoys are a lot more popular after Fixus Everythingus and after Lucius Malfoy's recent political shift, but there's still a lot of ugly history behind House Malfoy. They're even suspected of terrorist activities during the last war, and little actions can make a big difference.

I still have that first photo. Looking at it now, it sort of reminds me of a Muggle newspaper photo I once saw of the President of the United States with Vice President Clinton. I suppose that makes some sense. Professor Snape is continually praising Harry for his precocious Slytherin instincts, and what could be more Slytherin than politics? Besides, with the Minister of Magic as his mentor, why wouldn't Harry think like a politician?

Harry and Draco looked an awful lot like politicians on the telly as they entered the classroom, too. The students on the floor rearranging themselves to create a path and Draco strode forward, smiling and waving. Harry walked a few strides behind. I noticed that even though Draco watched in front, it was Harry that everybody was watching. When Draco moved to stand at the teachers' podium while Harry sat at the long table in front of the classroom I think the students shifted a little uneasily. I took a few more photos, and then sat down.

It was clear that the students wanted to hear from Harry, not Draco, but eventually they quieted down to listen anyway. I reckon he talked for about a quarter hour. He told about how he first became interested in Muggle Science (thanks to Harry) and how House Slytherin had changed the year before. He said that Chaos Club would be important because it would give Hogwarts students a chance to independently explore the principles of Muggle Science and to practice methods of thinking more rationally.

He also talked about his own previous prejudices against Muggle-borns and how his acquaintaince with Hermione Granger had challenged those beliefs even before he and Harry had used Muggle Science to analyse the concept of blood purity. Draco looked up shyly at Granger when he was explaining all this, and when I turned to look, she was looking down, her face a little reddened.

Draco added that we should not trust his word on the outcomes of his and Harry's research. The methods were simple, he said, and he'd love to see the experiment replicated. Repeatability, he said, was essential to Muggle Science. When he finally paused, several students took this as invitation to begin asking the questions on everybody's minds.

"How did Potter frighten that dementor, anyway?"

"Is it true that Minister Monroe wants to shut down Azkaban?"

"When do we get to learn how Fixus Everythingus works?"

And so on.

Draco turned to Harry, raised an eyebrow, and Harry stood to take the podium. I could feel the anticipation rising in the room, and seized the moment to stand up and take another photo. That one was a proper portrait of Harry. He held his head high and looked directly into the camera. He wasn't about to answer most of the student questions, though.

Instead, he spoke for a long time about Muggle technology. All very ordinary stuff to any child raised in a Muggle household, with Muggle television, but much of what he said appeared to be very impressive to the mostly Wizardborn audience. Harry told us about biomedicine, about electronics, and about space exploration. He spent a long time talking about the Moon Landing. I guess that the Moon Landing was a carefully selected topic, because that seemed to be what really got the attention of the students. It turns out that magic can't be used for space travel, and the idea that Muggles had done something that wizards couldn't was quite a shock for most of the audience. So Harry spoke for a long time about rocket technology, about the Russian satellite Sputnik and the race to the moon, and about the satellites that made weather-prediction possible.

When Harry got around to explaining about weather forecasting, George Weasley made a low remark that I didn't quite catch, something like, "Imagine, a type of Divination that actually works..." and several of the older students sitting near him snickered.

Luna, sitting next to me, stiffened. She glared at George but remained silent. Perhaps it should have occurred to me then that it had been a mistake to give her one of the Chaos Club cards, but I was too wrapped up in watching Harry working the crowd. His confidence was unreal, and he spoke just like a grown-up. He gave extremely simplified explanations of everything, though, and I began to realize how much work it must have been to figure out how to explain all of this in ways that wizard children could understand.

Eventually he concluded, "You see what Muggle scientists have been able to accomplish even without any capacity for magic. When combined with the power of Wizardry, the possibilities are unimaginable. As you all know from the changes to the Hogwarts curriculum, a few wizards are beginning to realize the opportunities already. But there's more to be done. Draco and I are asking you now to join us in this great endeavour."

I thought that speech of Harry's was totally brilliant. I couldn't wait to get into researching something really interesting like magic... not some silly school project on which fabric is most waterproof or anything boring like that, and I was so sure that Chaos Club was going to be huge. That's not quite the way things happened, though.

The next week the Slytherin classroom was half empty, and by the middle of the term, the group had shrunk down to just a few of us. There were some second years who knew Harry and Draco particularly well, the Weasley twins, and a few muggle-borns from various years. Granger and her attendants kept showing up as well, though a lot of the time it seems that Granger is just there to argue with Harry. As the group got smaller, things became more relaxed, though. At the start of each meeting, Draco casts a quick spell to arrange the chairs in a circle, and then we settle in to free-form discussion, or do a science experiment or rationality exercise that Harry and Draco have come up with.

It was just after one of the experiments that Luna finally decided she'd had enough of Chaos Club. I really shouldn't have been surprised that Chaos Club wasn't as good for Luna as it was for me. She has a lot of unusual ideas about the way magic works, and while Draco and Harry are always interested in new ideas, they always insist on the need for evidence. That idea doesn't quite fit into Luna's way of looking at things. Luna swears she isn't upset about it, but I feel bad about the way things went, and although Ginny still sometimes comes to the Chaos Club meetings with me, I don't think she'll ever really forgive Harry.
